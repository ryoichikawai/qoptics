5. The System#

The system we will investigate consists of four parts:

  1. Emitters of photons

  2. Free/thermal radiation field surrounding the emitters

  3. Driving laser field

  4. Single photon detectors

The followings are brief summery of each part. The detailed model will be introduced in separate subsections

5.1. Emitters#

The emitters of photons can be atoms, molecules, or quantum dots. Each of them are assumed to be much smaller than the wave length of light we are interested in. In this lecture note the individual emitter is modeled with a two-level system (TLS) or also known as two-state quantum system.

5.2. Free/thermal radiation field#

The free radiation field is a quantum version of electric field in a free space, defined with creation and annihilation operators of photons. The emitters are interacting with the radiation field and those in the excited state spontaneously emit photons. Furthermore, the dipole-dipole interaction between the two emitters is mediated by the radiation field. When the temperature of the field is not zero, the field is in thermal equilibrium and the emitters can be excited by absorbing thermal photons.

The emitters and the radiation fields form quantum entanglement though the interaction. The entanglement is of fundamental importance in the coherence theory.

5.3. Driving laser field#

The emitters are often driven by a laser beam. In order to reduce mathematical complication, we treat the laser beam as a classical time-dependent electric field, which is usually a good approximation for photons in coherent states.

5.4. Single photon detectors#

A the fundamental level, we still do not have clear understanding of quantum measurement of photons. We don’t have a mathematical model of the detecting process. We assume that when a photon arrives, the detector gets a “click” and record the arrival time. In other words, it can only count photons. When the click happens, we assume that quantum mechanical measurement took place and the quantum state of the whole system including the emitters collapses to a definite state corresponding the measurement outcome.

Unlike classical cases, we cannot measure the electric field since the field operator is not a physical observable. The individual click does not tell much other than the photon was there at that time. From the recorded data, we can extract certain physical quantity such as intensity with help of full counting statistics theory.
