7. Radiation field#

We have already define the free radiation field in Chapter 1. A brief summary is given here again. The key point in this chapter is the interaction between the emitter and the field.

7.1. Photon gas#

The free radiation field can have a finite temperature. We call it a photon gas. The free Hamiltonian of photon is defined by

(7.1)#\[ H_{R} = \sum_{\ell} \omega_{\ell} \left (a^{\dagger}_{\ell} a_{\ell} + \frac{1}{2} \right) \]

where \(a_{\ell}\) and \(a_{\ell}^{\dagger}\) are annihilation and creation operators of the \(\ell\)-th mode with momentum \(\mathbf{k}_{\ell}\) respectively (\(|\mathbf{k}_{\ell}| = \omega_{\ell}/c\)). We will omit the zero point energy in the rest of this note.

Initially the photon gas is assumed to be in a thermal equilibrium at temperature \(T\). The average occupation number follows the Planck distribution (Boltzman constant \(k_{B}=1\) is assumed)

\[ \langle a_{\ell}^{\dagger} a_{\ell}\rangle_{0} = \frac{1}{e^{\omega_{\ell}/T} - 1}. \]

When \(T=0\), \(\langle a_{\ell}^{\dagger} a_{\ell}\rangle_{0} =0\) for all modes.

When the photon gas interacts with the emitter, eventually the whole system reaches a new thermal equilibrium unless the emitter is externally driven.

7.2. Free radiation field#

We have defined the free radiation field as (See (1.5).)

(7.2)#\[ \mathbf{E}^{(+)}(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{\ell} \mathbf{e}_{\ell} \mathcal{E}_{\ell} a_{\ell} e^{i \mathbf{k}_{\ell} \cdot \mathbf{x}}, \qquad \mathbf{E}^{(-)}(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{\ell} \mathbf{e}_{\ell} \mathcal{E}^{*}_{\ell} a^{\dagger}_{\ell} e^{-i \mathbf{k}_{\ell} \cdot \mathbf{x}}. \]

where \(a^{\dagger}_{\ell}\) and \(a_{\ell}\) are creation and annihilation operators of photons in the plane wave mode \(e^{i \mathbf{k}_{\ell} \cdot \mathbf{x}}\) with linear polarization \(\epsilon_{\ell}\) and \(\mathcal{E}_{\ell} = i \sqrt{\frac{\hbar \omega_{\ell}}{2 \epsilon_{0} V}}\).

7.3. Interaction between emitter and radiation#

The interaction between the atoms and the radiation field is simply due to the force exerted by the radiation field on the electron. Assuming that the wave length of light emitted by the atom is much larger than the size of the atom itself, electric dipole approximation can be used and the interaction Hamiltonian can be written as

(7.3)#\[ H_{SR} = -\sum_{j}\mathbf{d}_j \cdot \mathbf{E}(\mathbf{x}_{j}) \]

where \(\mathbf{d}_{j}\) is the dipole moment operator of the \(j\)-th atom and \(\mathbf{x}_{j}\) is the center position of the atom. The dipole moment operator is defined by

(7.4)#\[ \mathbf{d}_{j} = \mathbf{d} \sigma^{+}_{j} + \mathbf{d}^{*} \sigma^{-}_{j} \]

with \(\mathbf{d} = - \langle e| \mathbf{p} |g\rangle\) where \(\mathbf{p}\) is the electric dipole moment of the atom, which is assumed to be identical to all the atoms.

Substituting Eq. (1.5), we have a full expression of the interaction Hamiltonian

(7.5)#\[ H_{SR} = - \sum_{j} \left[\mathbf{E}^{(+)}(\mathbf{x}_{j})+\mathbf{E}^{(-)}(\mathbf{x}_j)\right] \cdot \left( \mathbf{d} \sigma^{+}_{j} + \mathbf{d}^{*} \sigma^{-}_{j} \right) \]

Recall that \(E^{(-)}\) creates a photon and \(E^{(+)}\) destroys one, and that \(\sigma^{+}\) and \(\sigma^{-}\) represents excitation and deexcitation, respectively. Thenm \(E^{(-)} \sigma^{-}\) and \(E^{(+)} \sigma^{+}\) describe ordinary emission and absorption processes. However, Eq. (7.5) contains two more terms, \(E^{(-)} \sigma^{+}\) and \(E^{(+)} \sigma^{-}\) which do not make a sense from the perspective of classical physics. In quantum mechanics, emission and excitation can simultaneously happens despite the process does not conserve energy. These processes take place during very short period of time and the Heisenberg uncertainty principle allows non-energy conserving process as long as the energy is conserved in a longer time scale.

These virtual processes play key roles in various optical processes such as spontaneous parametric down-conversion and Raman scattering. Their controibution to regular optical processes is small and we ignore them under the name of rotating wave approximation. The resulting coupling Hamiltonian is

(7.6)#\[ H_{SR}^\text{RWA} = - \sum_{j} \left[\mathbf{d} \cdot \mathbf{E}^{(+)}(\mathbf{x}_{j})\, \sigma^{+}_{j} + \mathbf{d}^{*} \cdot \mathbf{E}^{(-)}(\mathbf{x}_j)\, \sigma^{-}_{j} \right] \]

7.4. Lamb shift and dipole-diploe interaction#

The above interaction generates spontaneous emission and absorption of photons. When the emitted photon is detected by a detector, the actual light is observed. There are other processes through the above interaction. Individual atoms can emit and absorb virtual photons. The atoms are said to be dressed with photons. A consequence is the shift of energy, known as the Lamb shift. Since the experimental value of the energy includes the Lamb shift, we will not add it here.

Another virtual processes is the exchange of photons between two atoms. Within the dipole approximation, it becomes the dipole-dipole interaction between atoms introduced in Chapter 6. Higher-order interaction is not considered in this note.

7.5. The radiation field generated by the emitters#

Suppose that the radiation is emitted at time \(t\) by the \(j\)-th emitter located at \(\mathbf{x}_{j}\). That means transition \(\sigma^{-}_{j}\) took place at \(t\). The detector is located at \(\mathbf{x}_{d}\). Let introduce a relative coordinate to the center of the quantum dot cluster. The detector position is \(\mathbf{R} = \mathbf{x}_d - \frac{1}{N}\sum_{j}\mathbf{x}_{j}\) and the location of \(i\)-th dot is \(\mathbf{r}_i = \mathbf{x}_{i} - \frac{1}{N}\sum_{j}\mathbf{x}_{j}\). We want to know the electric field that the detector interacts with at time \(t_R = t + R/c\). The net radiation field is a sum of a free field \(\mathbf{E}_{f}\) and a field generated by the source \(\mathbf{E}_{s}\). The free field is just the initial radiation field ((7.2)).

Lehmberg derived the source field by solving the Heisenberg equation of motion for \(a_{\ell}(t)\) with the Born-Markov approximation, which is the same approximation used to derive the quantum master equation. The result is

(7.7)#\[ \mathbf{E}^{(+)}_{s}(\mathbf{R},t_{R}) = - \frac{\omega_{0}^{2} d}{c^2} \sum_{j} \frac{ \left(\hat{\mathbf{d}} \times \hat{\mathbf{R}}_{j}\right) \times \hat{\mathbf{R}}_{j}}{R_{j}} \, e^{i k_0(R_{j}-R)} \sigma^{-}_{j}(t) \]

where \(k_{0} = \omega_{0}/c\) and \(\mathbf{R}_{j} = \mathbf{R} - \mathbf{r}_{j} = \mathbf{x}_{d} - \mathbf{x}_{j}\).

Since the detector is far from the emitter, we can assume \(|\mathbf{r}_{ij}| \ll \mathbf{R}_{j}|\), then Eq. (7.7) can be approximated to

(7.8)#\[ \mathbf{E}^{(+)}_{s}(\mathbf{R},t_{R}) \approx - \hat{\mathbf{e}}_{R} \frac{\omega_{0}^{2}d}{c^2} \left [ 1 - \left(\hat{\mathbf{d}}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{R}}\right)^2\right]^{1/2} \sum_{j} e^{- i k_{0} \hat{\mathbf{R}} \cdot \mathbf{r}_{j}} \sigma^{-}_{j}(t) \]

where \(\mathbf{e}_{R}\) is a unit vector in the direction of \(\left(\mathbf{d} \times \mathbf{R} \right)\times \mathbf{R}\). Notice that the field strength depends on the direction of the radial vector through the two inner products \(\hat{\mathbf{d}} \cdot \hat{\mathbf{R}}\) and \(\hat{\mathbf{R}} \cdot \mathbf{r}_{j}\). The latter depends the geometric structure of the quantum dot cluster.

Now the source field is expressed with the operators \(\sigma^{-}_{j}\) and \(\sigma^{+}_{j}\) instead of \(a_{\ell}\) and \(a^{\dagger}_{\ell}\). This allows us to evaluate the coherence functions with \(\sigma^{-}_{j}\) and \(\sigma^{+}_{j}\) which we can obtain from the quantum master equation.
