2. What is coherence?#

In classical optics, the origin of coherence was illusive. The classical theory uses artificial random phases to explain experimental observation without justification from the first principles. It is quantum mechanics that explains the origin of decoherence. In this chapter, first we introduce an operational definition of coherence. Then, we discuss the origin of decoherence with the standard quantum mechanics. At last, we make a connection between the quantum mechanical account and the operational definition.

2.1. Working definition of coherence#

The precise definition of quantum coherence has been difficult. People seems agreeing that coherence is the ability of a state to generate observable interference. As a working definition, we adopt this concept. For a comprehensive discussion on interference and coherence, see Ref. [4]

Definition 2.1 (Coherence)

Coherence is the ability of a state to generate observable interference.

Notice that the coherence is not the property of a system. It is a property of its state. Strictly speaking, statements like “These two atoms are incoherent” does not make a sense. It should be interpreted that the two atoms are in an incoherent state.

2.2. Mathematical expression#

How do we mathematically implement the above definition? The state of a quantum system is expressed as a density operator \(\rho\). How do we know if a density operator is capable of generating observable interference? We need to make a certain measurement to see interference.

Suppose that we measure an observable \(\hat{A}\) whose eigenvalues and the corresponding eigenvectors are expressed as \(a_{j}\) and \(|a_{j}\rangle\). Based on the Born rule, the probability of obtaining \(a_{1}\) upon measurement is given by

\[ P_{1} = \text{tr}\left(|a_{1}\rangle\langle a_{1}| \rho\right) = \langle a_{1}|\rho|a_{1}\rangle \]

This expression does not expose the presence of coherence. We need to introduce a basis set \(|u_{j}\rangle\). Then, the probability is written as

\[ P_{1} = \sum_{ij} \langle a_{1} | u_{i} \rangle \rho_{ij} \langle u_{j} | a_{1}\rangle = \sum_{j} |\langle a_{1}|u_{j}\rangle|^2 \rho_{jj} + \sum_{i\ne j} \langle a_{1} | u_{i} \rangle \langle u_{j} | a_{1}\rangle \rho_{ij} \]

where the density matrix is defined by \(\rho_{ij} = \langle u_{i} | \rho | u_{j}\rangle\). The second term in the right hand side is clearly showing interference between \(|u_{i}\rangle\) and \(|u_{j}\rangle,\, i\ne j\) through the off-diagonal elements \(\rho_{i\ne j}\). Therefore, mathematically speaking, whether the state has the ability to generate observable interference or not is determined by the off-diagonal elements of the density operator.

But it depends on the choice of the basis set. If a different basis set is used, the value of the off-diagonal elements change. Furthermore, we can always diagonalize a density matrix, eliminating all off-diagonal elements.

We conclude that coherence is the ability of a state to generate observable interference between certain basis vectors. The ability is determined by the off-diagonal elements of the density matrix in a specified basis set.

Coherence and basis set

It does not make a sense to discuss coherence without specifying a basis set.

2.3. Coherence vs superposition#

Interference is well-defined phenomena in the classical wave theory. In quantum mechanics, wave functions can generate interference exactly in the same way as the classical waves. It seems that any quantum state can generate interference and thus any quantum state seems coherent. This is indeed true if the state can be written in a superposition:

\[ |\psi\rangle = \sum_{j} c_{j} |u_{j}\rangle \]

Let us check if this state is coherent or not based on the working definition. Writing the state in terms of density operator

\[ \rho = |\psi\rangle \langle \psi | = \sum_{ij} c_{i} c^{*}_{j} |u_{i}\rangle \langle u_{j}| \]

The off-diagonal elements are \(\rho_{i\ne j} = c_{i} c^{*}_{j}\). All off-diagonal elements vanish if and only if \(c_{i}\ne 0\) and all other coefficients vanish, a state \(|\psi\rangle = |u_{i}\rangle\) cannot generate interference in the current basis set.[1] Otherwise, the state has the ability of generating interference and thus the state is coherent. Does this mean any quantum state is coherent?

The above analysis tells us that if the state of a system can be written as a superposition, then the system is coherent in that basis. No doubt about that. The question is if all states can be written as a superposition of basis vectors. The answer is No. A state that can be written as a state vector is called a pure state and the corresponding density operator is \(\rho = |\psi\rangle|\langle \psi |\). There are other states that cannot be expressed by a state vector. Those states are known as a mixed state. A mixed state can be also coherent even though it cannot be in a simple superposition state.

In conclusion, any pure state written as a superposition of basis vectors, it is highly coherent. However, not all states are pure. A mixed state can have a certain level of coherency as long as not all off-diagonal elements vanish. Do not forget that the matrix elements depend on the choice of a basis set. Until we pick a basis set, we can’t tell if the state is coherent or not.

2.4. The quantum origin of decoherence#

As we discuss above, the fundamental principles of quantum mechanics defines the state of a system as a vector which is inherently coherent in any basis. Then, how is the coherence lost? Actually, we never lose coherence at the fundamental level. Coherence appears lost only from a perspective of an observer. Indeed, the observer plays an essential role. When we observe the radiation field, we talk about the coherence of light. On the other hand, we also talk about the coherence of wave function in the atoms. There seems two different coherence depending on what we observe and they looks independent. As we see below, these two separate observations of coherence come from the same origin.

In order to make a story concrete and suitable for optics, we consider a simple example consisting of a pair of atoms and radiation field. The pair of atoms is one quantum system and the radiation field is another quantum system. The presence of multiple quantum systems is the key to understand coherence.

Each of atom has a ground state \(|g\rangle\) and an excited state \(|e\rangle\). We use the product basis

\[ |gg\rangle = |g\rangle \otimes |g\rangle, \quad |ge\rangle = |g\rangle \otimes |e\rangle, \quad |eg\rangle = |e\rangle \otimes |g\rangle, \quad |ee\rangle = |e\rangle \otimes |e\rangle \]

Now suppose that the atoms are initially in \(|ee\rangle\). Due to the interaction between the atoms and the radiation field, the system may evolve

(2.1)#\[ |\Psi\rangle = c_0 |ee\rangle \otimes |\phi_{0}\rangle + c_{1} |ge\rangle \otimes |\phi_{1}\rangle + c_{1} |eg\rangle \otimes |\phi_{1}^\prime\rangle + c_{2} |gg\rangle \otimes |\phi_{2}\rangle \]

where \(|\phi_{n}\rangle\) indicates the normalized state of field with \(n\) photon. The two states with a different number of photons are orthogonal, i.e., \(\langle\phi_{0}|\phi_{1}\rangle = \langle\phi_{0}|\phi_{2}\rangle = \langle\phi_{1}|\phi_{2}\rangle = 0\). The two states with the same number of photon, \(|\phi_{1}\rangle\) and \(|\phi_{1}^\prime\rangle\), represent two different states of the radiation field containing one photon. They are not necessarily orthogonal. Their difference depends on the distance between the emitters. When they are very close to each other, \(|\phi_{1}\rangle\) and \(|\phi_{1}^\prime\rangle\) becomes almost identical and thus \(\langle\phi_{1}|\phi_{1}^{\prime}\rangle \approx 1\). As the separation increases, the difference of two states becomes larger and \(\langle\phi_{1}|\phi_{1}^{\prime}\rangle\) eventually vanishes.

The type of superposition state (2.1) is known as a relative state. It is in general quantum mechanically entangled. There is one-to-one relation between the state of atoms and the state of radiation field.

The system may evolve to a different state

(2.2)#\[ |\Psi^\prime\rangle = c_0 |ee\rangle \otimes |\phi_{0}\rangle + \sqrt{2} c_{1} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|ge\rangle + |eg\rangle) \otimes \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} (|\phi_{1}\rangle +|\phi^{\prime}_{1}\rangle) + c_{2} |gg\rangle \otimes |\phi_{2}\rangle. \]

which has no entanglement within the subspace of one photon.

How does an observer see these two states?

2.4.1. Perspective of radiation field#

We make an observation of the radiation field through the interference experiment. In other words, we measure the intensity of light at position \(\mathbf{x}\).

For the first state (2.1), we find

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} |\Psi(x)|^{2} &= \text{tr}\left[(I \otimes | x \rangle\langle x |) |\Psi\rangle\langle\Psi|\right] \\ & = |c_{1}|^2 ( |\phi_{1}(x)|^2 + |\phi_{1}^\prime(x)|^2) + |c_{2}|^2 |\phi_{2}(x)|^2 \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

where \(|\phi_{n}(\mathbf{x}|^2\) is interpreted as the intensity \(\langle E^{(+}(\mathbf{x})E^{(-)}(\mathbf{x})\rangle\). There is no interference. Why? The interference term is

\[ \langle ge|eg\rangle (\phi^\prime_{1}(x))^{*} \phi_{2}(x) + \langle eg|ge\rangle (\phi^\prime_{2}(x))^{*} \phi_{1}(x) \]

which vanishes due to the orthogonality \(\langle ge|eg\rangle = \langle eg|ge\rangle = 0\). The observer concludes that the light is incoherent since no interference. Since the observer does not know the state of the atoms, the lack of interference is mysterious to them.

For the second state (2.2) we obtain

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} |\Psi^{\prime}(x)|^{2} &= \text{tr}\left[(I \otimes | x \rangle\langle x |) |\Psi^{\prime}\rangle\langle\Psi^{\prime}|\right] \\ & = |c_{1}|^2 |\phi_{1}(\mathbf{x})|^2 + |c_{1}|^2 |\phi_{1}^\prime(x)|^2) + |c_{1}|^2 \left( \phi_{1}(\mathbf{x})\phi_{1}^{\prime}(\mathbf{x}) + \phi_{1}(\mathbf{x})\phi_{1}^{\prime}(\mathbf{x}) \right) + |c_{2}|^2 |\phi_{2}(x)|^2 \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

which clearly shows the interference. The lack of entanglement allows it. However, the observer has no idea about it and declares that the radiation is coherent.

So what is the main difference between \(ge\rangle \otimes |\phi_{1}\rangle + |eg\rangle \otimes |\phi_{1}^\prime\rangle\) and \((|ge\rangle + |eg\rangle) \otimes (|\phi_{1}\rangle +|\phi^{\prime}_{1}\rangle)\) from the perspective of the radiation field. In the former state, the radiation field knows which atom emit the photon, whereas in the latter state the radiation field can’t say which. In optics literature, it is said that the detector cannot distinguish the source atoms.

2.4.2. Perspective of the atoms#

In the above example, the orthogonality of the emitter states destroyed the interference in the radiation field. Next we look at the state of atoms. Let us look at the interference of the atomic state through the measurement of \(\hat{A}\) discussed earlier. For the state \(|Psi\rangle\),

\[ P_1 = \text{tr} \left[ (|a_{1}\rangle\langle a_{1}| \otimes I) |\Psi\rangle\langle \Psi| \right] = |\langle a_{1}|ee\rangle|^2 + |\langle a_{1}|eg\rangle|^2 + |\langle a_{1}|ge\rangle|^2 + |\langle a_{1}|gg\rangle|^2 + \langle a_{1}|eg\rangle \langle ge| a_{1}\rangle \langle\phi_{1}^\prime |\phi_{1}\rangle + \langle a_{1}|ge\rangle \langle eg| a_{1}\rangle \langle\phi_{1} |\phi^\prime_{1}\rangle \]

The interference terms vanish if \(\rangle \langle\phi_{1} |\phi^\prime_{1}\rangle = 0\). At the opposit limit \(\rangle \langle\phi_{1} |\phi^\prime_{1}\rangle=1\), we have a perfect interference. The observer who are seeing only the atoms has no idea about the value of \(\rangle \langle\phi_{1} |\phi^\prime_{1}\rangle\). Therefore, coherence is quite mysterious to the observer.

Now, we look at the perfect coherence viewed from another basis set:

\[ |gg\rangle, \qquad |s\rangle \equiv \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \left(|ge\rangle + |eg\rangle\right), \qquad |a\rangle \equiv \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \left(|ge\rangle - |eg\rangle\right), \qquad |ee\rangle. \]

In this basis set, the state \(|Psi\rangle\) is expressed as

\[ |\Psi\rangle = c_0 |ee\rangle \otimes |\phi_{0}\rangle + c_{1} |s\rangle \otimes |\phi^{+}_{1}\rangle + c_{1} |a\rangle \otimes |\phi^{-}_{1}\rangle + c_{2} |gg\rangle \otimes |\phi_{2}\rangle \]

where

\[ |\phi^{\pm}_{1}\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \left |\phi_{1}\rangle \pm |\phi_{1}^\prime\rangle \right). \]

The condition for the perfect interference is \(\rangle \langle\phi_{1} |\phi^\prime_{1}\rangle=1\), which indicates \(|\phi^\prime_{1}\rangle = |\phi_{1}\rangle\). Then, a strange thing happens, \(|\phi^{-}_{1}\rangle = 0\) and the state \(|a\rangle\) completely disappears from \(|\Psi\rangle\). That means the state \(|a\rangle\) does not interact with the radiation field! It is called a dark state.

Look at the state vector again without \(|a\rangle\),

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} |\Psi\rangle &= c_0 |ee\rangle \otimes |\phi_{0}\rangle + c_{1} |s\rangle \otimes |\phi^{+}_{1}\rangle + c_{2} |gg\rangle \otimes |\phi_{2}\rangle\\ &= c_0 |ee\rangle \otimes |\phi_{0}\rangle + c_{1} (|ge\rangle + |eg\rangle) \otimes |\phi_{1}\rangle + c_{2} |gg\rangle \otimes |\phi_{2}\rangle \\ &= |\Psi^\prime\rangle \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

The second example state \(|\Psi^\prime\rangle\) is just a special case of the first one \(|\Psi\rangle\) with \(|\phi^\prime_{1}\rangle = |\phi_{1}\rangle\).

Coherence and observer

The concept of coherence/decoherece arises when an observer looks at only a subsystem of a larger system. The origin of decoherence is not in the subsystem itself. Quantum entanglement between the subsystem and the rest of th world causes decoherence apprent to observers in the subsystem.

2.5. Reduced density operator for a subsystem#

Based on the the working definition, coherence is mathematically measured by the off-diagonal element of the density matrix. But this density matrix is viewed by the observer, not the one for the whole system. How can we reconcile the two views of coherency, the local view vs the global view if you will. There is a mathematical method to find the density operator of a subsystem from the density operator of the whole system. Let us continue the previous example.

The density operator of the whole system is simply \(\rho_{AR} = |\Psi\rangle\langle \Psi|\) where \(A\) and \(R\) stand for the atoms and the radiation field, respectively. Mathematically speaking the state of a subsystem is defined by a partial trace. [5]:

\[ \rho_{A} = \text{tr}_{R} \rho_{AR} = \sum_{j} \left(I_{A} \otimes \langle u_{j} | \right) \rho_{AR} \left(I_{A} \otimes | u_{j} \rangle \right) \]

where \(I_{A}\) is the identity operator in the Hilbert space for A and \(|u_{j}\rangle\) are orthonormal basis vectors in the Hilbert space for R. Any basis set can be used. Performing the partial trace, we find

\[ \rho_{A} = |c_{0}|^{2} |ee\rangle\langle ee| + |c_{1}|^2|eg\rangle\langle eg| + |c_{1}|^2|ge\rangle\langle ge| + |c_{2}|^2|gg\rangle\langle gg| + |c_{1}|^{2} \langle \phi_{1}|\phi_{1}^\prime\rangle |eg\rangle\langle ge| + |c_{1}|^{2} \langle \phi_{1}^\prime|\phi_{1}\rangle |ge\rangle\langle eg| \]

or writing in a matrix form with the product basis,

\[\begin{split} \rho = \begin{pmatrix} |c_{0}|^{2} & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & |c_{1}|^{2} & |c_{1}|^{2} \langle \phi_{1}|\phi_{1}^\prime\rangle & 0 \\ 0 & |c_{1}|^{2} \langle \phi_{1}^\prime|\phi_{1}\rangle & |c_{1}|^{2} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & |c_{2}|^{2} \end{pmatrix} \end{split}\]

The off-diagonal element \(\rho_{eg,ge} = |c_{1}|^{2} \langle \phi_{1}|\phi_{1}^\prime\rangle\) completely vanishes if \(\langle \phi_{1}|\phi_{1}^\prime\rangle=0\). When \(\langle \phi_{1}|\phi_{1}^\prime\rangle=1\) we have a perfect coherence. This is exactly what we have already seen in the above discussion. Hence, the off-diagonal elements of the reduced density operator retain some information of the radiation field and tell us if the radiation field distinguish the two atoms or not.

In conclusion, the working definition of coherence does not tell the origin of decoherence but it is consistent with the global view of coherence.

For detailed discussion on decoherence beyond optics, see Refs. [6, 7, 8].

2.6. Methods of open quantum system#

One of the objective of the present lecture note is to calculate the reduced density matrix for the emitters by tracing out the radiation degrees of freedom. How can we calculate the reduced density in practical applications? Do we have to compute the while system and take a partial trace? That needs substantial mathematical and computational power. We cannot use the traditional quantum mechanics for a subsystem since it is developed only for a closed system.

Fortunately, a method of open quantum system has been developed. Although it is an approximated method and valid only in certain situation, there are many evidences that the method works quite well for quantum optics. The method will be introduced in Chapter 12.

2.7. Types of Decoherence#

There are several type of decoherence from the local perspective. Here we shows two types relevant to our discussion.

2.7.1. Dissipation#

The energy exchange between the system and environment causes the dissipation. If the atoms are interacting with a thermal radiation field. Their state quickly settles in thermal equilibrium (due to entanglement between the system and the radiation field).
Hence, there is no coherence between \(|g\rangle\) and \(|e\rangle\). The density matrix decays as

\[ \rho_{jj}(t) = \rho^\text{eq}_{jj} + \left(\rho_{jj}(0) - \rho^\text{eq}_{jj}\right) e^{-t/T_{1}}, \qquad \rho_{i\ne j}(t) = \rho_{i\ne j}(0) e^{-t/T_{2}} \]

where \(\rho_{jj}^\text{eq}\) is the diagonal element of the equilibrium state. \(T_{1}\) and \(T_{2}\) are relaxation time for diagonal and off-diagonal elements.

The disappearance of the off-diagonal elements seems strange. Without the off-diagonal elements there seems no transition between the orbitals. Even then the quantum dots are emitting light! So, how does the system go up and down between \(|g\rangle\) and \(|e\rangle\)? Recall that the quantum mechanics is probabilistic. It cannot tell what individual systems are doing. It can tell only what the ensemble of the systems is doing. Even when the off-diagonal element is empty, individual systems can have a superposition state for a brief period, which allows the transition and emission of light. However, the past memory is quickly lost and the transition happens at random. Hence, the phase of the off-diagonal is random. When the ensemble average is taken, the off-diagonal elements vanish due to the random phase. Because of it, the light emitted from the thermal state is not coherent. (Recall that in classical optics, random phase is attached to the radiation field to mimic this effect.See Chapter 1.) Since the dissipation is a part of the optical processes, we cannot remove this decoherence. Our concern is how long the atoms can stay coherent.

2.7.2. Pure dephasing#

Dephasing is another type of decoherence. In the pure dephasing, the off-diagonal elements decay but the diagonal elements survives. They decays as

\[ \rho_{jj}(t) = \rho_{jj}(0), \qquad \rho_{i\ne j} (t) = \rho_{i\ne j} (0) e^{-t/T_{\phi}} \]

where \(T_{\phi}\) is the decay time due to the pure dephasing.

If the emitter is imbedded in a solid, it interacts with phonon. The scattering of phonon causes the pure dephasing. If we need to take into account the phonon assisted dephasing, we add a acoustic phonon gas as a second environment. The effect seems week but we shall consider it in certain cases.

In general the decay times are related as

(2.3)#\[ \frac{1}{T_{2}} = \frac{1}{2T_{1}} + \frac{1}{T_{\phi}} \]

In the absence of the dephasing, \(T_{2} = 2 T_{1}\), which we will discuss in case studies.

[1]

If there is only one nonvanishing diagonal element, the state is pure and we have no way to determine coherency from it.
